In Morocco, tea is not just a drink; it is a sign of hospitality, friendship, and tradition. Known locally as "Atay," Moroccan mint tea is served at all times of the day and is the first thing offered to any guest.

## A Symbol of Hospitality

The preparation of tea, often referred to as "atai," is a ceremonial art form passed down through generations. It is typically prepared by the head of the family and served to guests as a gesture of welcome. Refusing a cup of tea can be considered impolite, as it is a rejection of the host's hospitality.

## The Ingredients

Authentic Moroccan tea requires three simple but specific ingredients:

- **Gunpowder Green Tea:** A type of Chinese green tea where each leaf has been rolled into a small round pellet
- **Fresh Mint:** Generous bunches of fresh spearmint (nana mint) are essential for the signature flavor
- **Sugar:** Moroccans have a sweet tooth, and the tea is traditionally very sweet, often using large sugar cones

## The Pouring Ritual

Perhaps the most famous part of the tea ceremony is the pouring. The tea is poured from a height of at least 12 inches (30 cm) into small glass cups. This is not just for show; it serves two purposes:

1. It aerates the tea, enhancing the flavor
2. It creates a foam on top of the tea, which is considered a sign of a good brew

The pouring is done with great skill and precision, and watching an experienced tea maker is truly mesmerizing.
